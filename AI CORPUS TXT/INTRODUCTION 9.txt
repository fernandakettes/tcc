INTRODUCTION 9

The landscape of music education has undergone significant transformation over the past several decades, moving away from a predominantly Western classical canon toward a more inclusive and culturally diverse pedagogical approach. This shift reflects broader societal changes and an increasing recognition that music, as a universal human phenomenon, manifests in countless forms across different cultures, each with its own aesthetic values, performance practices, and social meanings. Ethnomusicology, the study of music in its cultural context, has emerged as a crucial discipline that challenges music educators to reconsider fundamental assumptions about what music is, how it should be taught, and whose musical traditions deserve space in formal educational settings. By integrating ethnomusicological perspectives into music education, educators can foster not only musical competence but also cultural awareness, critical thinking, and respect for diversity. This paper examines how ethnomusicological principles can reshape music education practices, exploring the theoretical foundations that connect these two fields and investigating practical strategies for implementing culturally responsive pedagogy in contemporary music classrooms.
The relationship between ethnomusicology and music education is both natural and complex, rooted in shared concerns about musical transmission, learning processes, and the social functions of music-making. Traditional music education often emphasized notation-based learning, technical proficiency, and the mastery of a relatively narrow repertoire, primarily drawn from European art music traditions. However, ethnomusicological research has demonstrated that the majority of the world's musical cultures rely on oral transmission, emphasize communal participation over individual virtuosity, and integrate music deeply into daily life rather than treating it as a separate aesthetic domain. These insights challenge educators to expand their pedagogical toolkit beyond conventional methods and to recognize that different musical traditions may require different approaches to teaching and learning. Furthermore, ethnomusicology's commitment to understanding music as a reflection of cultural identity, social organization, and historical experience provides educators with frameworks for addressing issues of representation, authenticity, and cultural appropriation in the classroom. As student populations become increasingly diverse and globalized, the need for music education that honors multiple musical traditions and promotes intercultural dialogue has never been more pressing.
Implementing ethnomusicologically informed music education presents both opportunities and challenges for contemporary educators. On one hand, exposure to diverse musical traditions can enhance students' creativity, broaden their aesthetic appreciation, and develop their capacity for cultural empathy and global citizenship. Learning to perform gamelan music, participate in West African drumming ensembles, or understand the improvisational principles of Indian classical music can provide students with embodied knowledge of different ways of organizing sound, time, and social interaction. On the other hand, educators must navigate complex questions about how to teach music from cultures that may be unfamiliar to them, how to avoid superficial or stereotypical representations, and how to balance breadth of exposure with depth of understanding. This paper argues that successful integration of ethnomusicology into music education requires ongoing teacher education, collaboration with culture bearers and community musicians, and a willingness to decenter Western musical practices as the default or superior model. By examining case studies, pedagogical approaches, and theoretical debates at the intersection of these two fields, this study aims to contribute to the ongoing conversation about creating more equitable, relevant, and culturally sustaining music education programs.
